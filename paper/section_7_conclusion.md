## 7 Conclusion

We present the first weight-only 2-bit quantization of OLMoE-1B-7B, achieved entirely on a single 12 GB consumer laptop GPU. The method is a minimal modification to QTIP's BlockLDLQ pipeline — substitute per-expert routing-conditioned Hessians for per-layer aggregated Hessians — and requires no algorithmic changes to the trellis quantizer, codebook, or LDL decomposition. On OLMoE-1B-7B-0125, we achieve 9.09 WikiText-2 perplexity and 14.16 C4 perplexity at 2 bits per weight (1.367x and 1.157x the fp16 baseline), and retain 71.15% HellaSwag accuracy, 77.97% PIQA accuracy, and 44.28% ARC-Challenge accuracy on zero-shot evaluation. These numbers are competitive with the strongest published 3-bit MoE quantization results — EAQuant [Fu et al., 2026] operates in the W3A4 regime while ours is W2A16 — despite operating at one bit lower per weight.

The paper makes three contributions of varying scope:

Routing-conditioned per-expert Hessian calibration (Sections 3.2, 5.1) is the natural calibration unit for routed-MoE architectures. Collecting a separate input Hessian from only the tokens each expert actually receives yields a small but consistent 0.09–0.28 PPL improvement over per-layer alternatives — both unweighted and token-weighted — indicating that routing specialization carries distributional signal that any per-layer summary collapses.
LUT fine-tuning is unnecessary (Section 5.3) when paired with per-expert Hessians. Across four learning rates, LUT fine-tuning yields 3.0% MSE reduction on expert weights and 0.0% on attention weights, below the perplexity noise floor. The QTIP HYB codebook is already at or near the local optimum given per-expert calibration; no adaptive codebook adjustment is needed.
Post-RHT MoE expert weights are numerically Gaussian (Section 5.4). We measure mean kurtosis 3.004 across 3072 expert weight matrices, with standard deviation 0.014 across tensors, providing the first direct numerical confirmation of QTIP's Gaussianity premise on a class of weights the original QTIP paper did not evaluate. This explains why per-expert calibration yields only small additional quality — the codebook-quantizer pair is already near-optimal — and why LUT fine-tuning provides no improvement.

Together, these contributions establish that 2-bit MoE quantization with quality preservation is achievable without datacenter compute, without algorithmic novelty, and without codebook-level adaptation. The limitation is runtime: we currently dequantize to bf16 at install time, so the 8x on-disk compression does not translate to inference-time memory savings. Native 2-bit inference kernels for OLMoE — the natural next step and the subject of planned follow-up work — would close this gap and deliver both the memory and the throughput benefits that 2-bit weight compression theoretically enables.
The broader methodological lesson: when a quantization algorithm relies on a distributional assumption (here, approximate Gaussianity after RHT), MoE architectures do not require new quantization algorithms — they require recognizing that the calibration unit should follow the routing topology. The algorithm is unchanged; only the granularity of Hessian collection needs to match the model's architectural granularity. We expect this pattern to generalize beyond OLMoE to other fine-grained MoE architectures.
