## 1 Introduction

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) language models have emerged as the dominant scaling strategy for open-source language models beyond the dense-transformer regime, with architectures such as Mixtral-8x7B [Jiang et al., 2024], DeepSeek-MoE [Dai et al., 2024], Qwen-MoE [Qwen, 2024], and OLMoE [Muennighoff et al., 2024] achieving competitive quality at a fraction of the active parameter count. Their efficiency advantage during compute, however, comes with a deployment tax: the full expert pool must be resident in memory even though only a sparse subset is active per token. For OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 — a 7-billion-parameter MoE with 1.3 billion active parameters per token — the bf16 checkpoint occupies roughly 26 GB on disk and ~14 GB of active weight memory at inference time. Neither fits comfortably on consumer-grade laptop GPUs, which typically ship with 8-16 GB of VRAM. Weight quantization is the standard response.

Post-training quantization (PTQ) of dense LLMs is well-studied at 4-bit (GPTQ [Frantar et al., 2023], AWQ [Lin et al., 2023]) and has been pushed to 2-bit through vector and trellis-coded methods (QuIP# [Tseng et al., 2024], AQLM [Egiazarian et al., 2024], QTIP [Tseng et al., 2024]). MoE-specific PTQ has trailed dense quantization by approximately one bit per weight: the most recent published results — MoEQuant [Hu et al., 2025], MoPEQ [Chitty-Venkata et al., 2025], and EAQuant [Fu et al., 2026] — operate at 3-4 bits per weight, with only EAQuant attempting 2-bit weight quantization and only in a joint weight-activation regime (W2A4, which shows catastrophic degradation without additional techniques). The open question is whether pure weight-only 2-bit quantization of MoE models can preserve quality — and if so, whether it can be done outside the datacenter-compute regime that characterizes most quantization research.

We answer both questions affirmatively. We compress OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 to 3.3 GB on disk at 2 bits per expert weight (an 8x disk compression) using a minimal modification to QTIP's BlockLDLQ trellis quantization pipeline, achieving WikiText-2 perplexity 9.09 and C4 perplexity 14.16 against fp16 baselines of 6.65 and 12.24. The entire research pipeline — Hessian collection over 2.1M calibration tokens, BlockLDLQ quantization of all 3136 targets, and full perplexity and zero-shot benchmark evaluation — runs end-to-end on a single NVIDIA RTX 4080 Laptop GPU with 12 GB of VRAM, inside Windows Subsystem for Linux on a consumer laptop, in under 12 hours wall time. No multi-GPU, no datacenter accelerators, no cloud compute.

The key methodological observation is that **MoE routing makes the per-layer Hessian the wrong calibration unit**. The error-compensation framework underlying BlockLDLQ, GPTQ, and their descendants assumes that each quantization target — each weight matrix — has a single well-defined input distribution, summarized by an input Hessian H = E[xx^T] collected from calibration data. For dense LLMs this is uncontroversial: every token passes through every weight matrix. For MoE models, the router dispatches each token to only k of N experts (k=8 of N=64 in OLMoE) based on learned token-expert affinity. Every expert therefore sees a *different* subset of the calibration distribution, and the imbalance is substantial: in our OLMoE calibration sample, the ratio of the most-routed expert to the least-routed expert varies from 2.6x at layer 0 to 17.4x at layer 15, with a mean of 8.9x across the 16 layers. Collapsing these 64 distinct per-expert input distributions into a single per-layer summary — whether by unweighted averaging, token-count weighting, or ignoring routing entirely during calibration — discards the distributional signal that distinguishes experts from one another. Existing MoE quantization methods do precisely this: MoEQuant modifies the Hessian's token weighting, MoPEQ uses Hessian trace as a sensitivity metric for bitwidth allocation, and EAQuant addresses calibration data imbalance via oversampling, but none of them collect distinct per-expert input Hessians from routed activations.

We collect them. Our method installs a per-instance monkey patch on OlmoeExperts.forward that mirrors the original computation by construction while accumulating per-expert input activations into distinct Hessian buffers — one gate-up Hessian and one down-projection Hessian for each of the 64 experts in each of the 16 layers, for 2048 distinct expert Hessians in total. These per-expert Hessians are fed into an otherwise unmodified QTIP BlockLDLQ pipeline with random Hadamard transform preprocessing, hybrid (HYB) bitshift codebook, and Viterbi decoding at Tx = Ty = 16 block size and trellis vector dimension V = 2. The only departure from QTIP's dense-LLM setup is which Hessian each expert target sees during quantization. Everything else — the codebook, the LDL decomposition, the error propagation, the bitstream format — is unchanged.

**Contributions.** This paper makes three contributions of varying scope:

1. **Routing-conditioned per-expert Hessian calibration** is the natural calibration unit for routed-MoE architectures. On OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 at 2-bit weight-only quantization, per-expert Hessian calibration consistently outperforms both unweighted and token-weighted per-layer Hessian baselines by 0.09-0.12 PPL on WikiText-2 and 0.27-0.28 PPL on C4. The gap persists against the token-weighted baseline — which matches the implicit calibration of MoEQuant, MoPEQ, and EAQuant — indicating that routing *specialization* (what each expert does with its routed tokens) carries distributional signal that any per-layer aggregation, weighted or unweighted, necessarily collapses (Sections 3.2, 5.1).

2. **LUT fine-tuning is unnecessary** when paired with per-expert Hessian calibration. Across four learning rates, fine-tuning the QTIP HYB codebook yields 3.0% MSE reduction on expert weights and 0.0% on attention weights — well below the perplexity noise floor. This is a clean negative result: the QTIP-released codebook trained on i.i.d. Gaussian samples is already at or near the local optimum given per-expert calibration, and no adaptive codebook adjustment is needed (Section 5.3).

3. **Post-RHT MoE expert weights are numerically Gaussian.** We measure mean excess kurtosis 3.004 across all 3072 expert weight matrices in OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 after applying the same random Hadamard transform used in the quantization pipeline, with standard deviation 0.01 across tensors — within 0.4% of the Gaussian reference. This is the first direct numerical confirmation of QTIP's Gaussianity premise on a class of weights the original QTIP paper did not evaluate, and it provides a quantitative explanation for why per-expert calibration yields only small additional quality improvements and why LUT fine-tuning yields none: the codebook-quantizer pair is already near the rate-distortion limit for the distribution it sees (Section 5.4).

Together, these contributions establish that 2-bit weight-only MoE quantization with quality preservation is achievable without datacenter compute, without algorithmic novelty, and without codebook-level adaptation. The primary remaining limitation is runtime: we currently dequantize the 2-bit bitstream to bf16 at install time, so the 8x on-disk compression does not translate to inference-time memory savings or throughput improvements. QTIP already demonstrates native 2-bit inference kernels for dense Llama-family models achieving >3x speedup over fp16 [Tseng et al., 2024, Table 4]; porting these kernels to MoE's routed-expert layout is the natural next step and is the subject of planned follow-up work.

**Positioning.** Our contribution is not algorithmic novelty. BlockLDLQ, RHT, and the QTIP HYB codebook are existing components from prior work, used here exactly as published. The contribution is methodological clarity about the right calibration unit for routed-MoE architectures, paired with the empirical demonstration that 2-bit MoE quality preservation is achievable entirely on consumer hardware. To our knowledge, this paper reports the first weight-only 2-bit quantization results on OLMoE in the W2A16 regime, and the first 2-bit MoE quantization results obtained on a single 12 GB consumer laptop GPU.
