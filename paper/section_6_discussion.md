## 6 Discussion

### 6.1 What the Per-Expert Contribution Buys You

The ablation in Section 5.1 shows that per-expert Hessian calibration yields a 0.09-0.12 PPL improvement on WikiText-2 and a 0.27-0.28 PPL improvement on C4 compared to per-layer Hessian baselines, with consistent direction across both unweighted and token-weighted per-layer baselines. These gaps are small in absolute terms — about 1% of the fp16-to-2-bit PPL gap on WikiText-2 — but they are reproducible across datasets and baseline choices, and they come at zero additional inference cost. The per-expert contribution is best understood not as a "large quality improvement" but as methodological clarity about the natural calibration unit for routed-MoE architectures. When routing dispatches each expert to a different subset of the input distribution, per-expert calibration recovers a distributional signal that any per-layer summary — weighted or unweighted — necessarily collapses.

The fact that the token-weighted per-layer baseline (which matches the implicit calibration of MoEQuant, MoPEQ, and EAQuant) does not catch up to per-expert calibration is itself informative. If routing frequency alone were the signal, then weighting by n_e during the Hessian collapse would recover most of the gap, since high-traffic experts would contribute proportionally more. Instead, the unweighted and weighted baselines give nearly identical C4 PPL (14.43 vs. 14.44), and both sit clearly below per-expert (14.16). This suggests that routing *specialization* — what each expert actually does with its routed tokens — is the real signal, not just routing *frequency*. Token-weighted aggregation captures frequency but not specialization; per-expert calibration captures both.

### 6.2 Why Trellis Quantization Works for MoE: The Gaussian Story

Section 5.4 measures that post-RHT expert weights have mean kurtosis 3.004 across all 3072 expert matrices in OLMoE-1B-7B, with standard deviation 0.01 across tensors. This is not a small deviation from Gaussianity — it is essentially numerical equality. The random Hadamard transform collapses whatever distributional structure MoE routing-conditioned training induces and produces weights that are indistinguishable from i.i.d. Gaussian at the level of marginal moments. This validates QTIP's core design premise (that trellis-coded quantization on RHT'd weights is a Gaussian source coding problem) on a new class of models that QTIP itself did not evaluate.

The practical consequence is that **no adaptation of the QTIP codebook is necessary for MoE quantization**. The HYB code trained on i.i.d. Gaussian samples is already matched to the distribution it sees on OLMoE expert weights post-RHT. This is consistent with the LUT fine-tuning negative result in Section 5.3: there is no remaining error structure for codebook adjustment to recover, because the distribution the codebook sees is the distribution the codebook was designed for. Per-expert Hessian calibration operates on top of this already-near-optimal quantizer and extracts a small additional quality increment from the per-expert input statistics, not from any codebook-level adaptation.

### 6.3 Consumer Hardware Feasibility

All experiments in this paper — Hessian collection across 2.1M calibration tokens, BlockLDLQ quantization of all 3136 targets, and full perplexity and zero-shot benchmark evaluation — were performed on a single NVIDIA RTX 4080 Laptop GPU with 12 GB of VRAM, inside WSL2 on a consumer Windows laptop. No multi-GPU, no datacenter accelerators, no cloud compute. The Hessian collection pass takes ~3 hours, quantization takes ~3.5 hours for all 16 layers, and PPL evaluation takes ~10 minutes per dataset. The total end-to-end wall time from a fresh checkpoint to fully evaluated 2-bit numbers is under 12 hours.

This matters for two reasons. First, it lowers the barrier to entry for MoE quantization research: independent researchers and small labs can now iterate on calibration strategies, codebook choices, and routing-aware quantization tricks without needing datacenter access. Second, it demonstrates that the quality-preservation half of the MoE-on-consumer-hardware problem is solved at 2 bits — what remains is the runtime half, which we discuss next.

### 6.4 Limitations

**Runtime does not match on-disk size.** Our 2-bit bitstream occupies 3.3 GB on disk versus 26 GB for the bf16 checkpoint (an 8x compression), but at inference time we currently dequantize the bitstream back to bf16 before loading it into the model. The runtime memory footprint is therefore ~14 GB (bf16 weights, same as the original model), not 3.3 GB. On a 12 GB VRAM device, this means the transformers device_map automatically offloads a subset of layers to CPU RAM; our reported PPL and lm-eval numbers are measured with this CPU/GPU split happening implicitly. Native 2-bit inference kernels for OLMoE would eliminate this dequant-to-fp16 install path and allow the model to fit entirely in GPU memory, providing both memory savings and inference speedups via reduced memory bandwidth. QTIP already demonstrates native 2-bit kernels for dense Llama-family models achieving >3x speedup over fp16 [Tseng et al., 2024, Table 4]; porting these kernels to MoE's routed-expert layout is an engineering task, not a research risk, and we leave it to follow-up work (Paper 2 in our planned sequence).

**Single model evaluated.** All results in this paper are on OLMoE-1B-7B-0125. We did not evaluate on other MoE architectures such as Mixtral-8x7B, DeepSeek-MoE-16B, or Qwen-MoE-14B. OLMoE was selected for its open-source status (weights, training data, and evaluation code all publicly released), its fine-grained expert architecture (64 experts per layer, top-8 routing — the most routing-heavy mainstream MoE), and its parameter footprint which fits our 12 GB VRAM target after quantization. The core method — per-expert Hessian collection, RHT, BlockLDLQ — is architecture-agnostic and should transfer to other MoE models without modification. We leave multi-model evaluation to future work.

**Weight-only quantization.** Our method quantizes only the weight tensors. Activations remain in bf16 throughout inference. Joint weight-and-activation quantization (as in DuQuant, EAQuant, FlatQuant) is a harder problem that introduces activation outlier suppression, router quantization sensitivity, and activation-memory pressure as additional concerns. We view weight-only 2-bit as a natural first milestone for MoE quantization on consumer hardware; weight-and-activation joint quantization at 2 bits for MoE is an open problem.

**Calibration data.** We calibrate on 2.1M tokens sampled from the C4 English training set. OLMoE's actual pretraining distribution (Dolma) was unavailable to us during calibration due to dataset access issues; C4 is a reasonable proxy (it is a major component of Dolma and a standard PTQ calibration corpus), but calibrating on the exact pretraining mixture could further reduce the quantization gap. We leave Dolma-based calibration as a straightforward future experiment.

**Per-layer baselines not evaluated on downstream tasks.** Section 5.2 reports downstream task results (HellaSwag, ARC-Challenge, ARC-Easy, PIQA, WikiText word-PPL) only for the per-expert configuration, not for the per-layer baselines. The per-expert vs. per-layer ablation therefore rests entirely on PPL. Given the small PPL gaps observed (0.09-0.28 PPL), we expect the downstream task gaps to be correspondingly small and within benchmark noise, but we have not measured this directly. Running lm-evaluation-harness on the per-layer baselines is a ~5-hour evaluation per configuration and is deferred to the camera-ready version of this paper.

**No quantization of the router or embedding tables.** We quantize attention and MLP expert weights but leave the router (a 2048 x 64 linear layer per MoE block) and the token embedding and LM head tables in bf16. The router is negligible in size (~2 MB per layer) but sensitive to quantization perturbation [EAQuant, Fu et al. 2026]; the embedding and LM head together account for ~1 GB and can be quantized independently if desired. Our reported 3.3 GB figure is therefore the expert+attention contribution; the full model with bf16 router and embeddings is somewhat larger on disk (approximately 4.5 GB total when bf16 router + embeddings are included). This matches the regime of all prior MoE quantization work, which typically keeps router and embeddings in higher precision.
