## 5 Experiments and Results

### 5.1 Main Results: Language Modeling Perplexity

We report perplexity on WikiText-2 and C4 for OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 in three configurations: fp16 baseline, our 2-bit quantization with per-expert Hessians, and two 2-bit baselines using per-layer Hessians (unweighted and token-weighted means of the per-expert Hessians, as defined in Section 3.2). All quantized configurations share identical attention quantization, RHT preprocessing, BlockLDLQ algorithm, and trellis codebook; the only difference is which Hessian is used for the expert weight quantization.

**Table 1: Perplexity on WikiText-2 and C4 at 2 bits per weight.**

| Configuration | WikiText-2 PPL | C4 PPL | wt2 ratio | c4 ratio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| fp16 baseline | 6.65 | 12.24 | 1.000x | 1.000x |
| 2-bit, per-expert H (ours) | **9.09** | **14.16** | **1.367x** | **1.157x** |
| 2-bit, per-layer H (unweighted mean) | 9.21 | 14.43 | 1.385x | 1.179x |
| 2-bit, per-layer H (token-weighted mean) | 9.18 | 14.44 | 1.381x | 1.180x |

The per-expert configuration achieves WikiText-2 perplexity 9.09 against an fp16 baseline of 6.65 — a 1.367x ratio at one-eighth the original bitrate. On C4, the fp16 ratio is tighter at 1.157x. Both ratios are substantially closer to 1.0 than would be expected for a naive 2-bit quantization of a dense LLM of comparable active-parameter count, and (as we argue in Section 5.4) reflect the good match between OLMoE's post-RHT expert weight distribution and the trellis codebook.

**Per-expert vs per-layer ablation.** The per-expert Hessian configuration consistently outperforms both per-layer baselines on both datasets. Against the unweighted-mean baseline, the gap is 0.12 PPL on WikiText-2 and 0.27 PPL on C4. Against the token-weighted mean — the more standard comparison, mathematically equivalent to recollecting Hessians without partitioning by routing, which is the implicit calibration used by MoEQuant, MoPEQ, and EAQuant — the gap is 0.09 PPL on WikiText-2 and 0.28 PPL on C4. Notably, the gap against the weighted baseline on C4 (0.28) is essentially the same as the gap against the unweighted baseline on C4 (0.27), indicating that the per-expert contribution is not an artifact of the specific baseline averaging scheme: the routing-conditioned signal captured by per-expert Hessians is distinct from what either per-layer summary captures.

The gaps are modest in absolute terms but consistent across datasets and across the per-layer breakdown. The C4 gap is roughly twice the WikiText-2 gap, reflecting both the larger evaluation set (lower variance) and the broader text distribution (more sensitive to the routing-conditioned input statistics that per-expert calibration captures). We interpret this as evidence that per-expert calibration provides a real but bounded improvement: the marginal gain from routing-conditioned Hessians is small because BlockLDLQ with any reasonable Hessian already captures most of the achievable 2-bit representational capacity (Section 5.3), but the gain is reproducible and grows with the evaluation set's distributional breadth.

**Comparison to prior MoE quantization.** EAQuant [Fu et al., 2026] is the only prior MoE quantization work that reports results on the OLMoE architecture (specifically the OLMoE-7B-0924 checkpoint). Because the two papers use different OLMoE training recipes (0924 vs. 0125), absolute PPL values are not directly comparable; we compare fp16 ratios instead. At weight-only 3-bit quantization (W3A16), EAQuant achieves 1.282x wt2 ratio and 1.177x c4 ratio on OLMoE-7B-0924 (9.60 wt2 / 12.38 c4 against fp16 7.49 / 10.52). Our weight-only 2-bit method achieves 1.367x wt2 ratio and 1.157x c4 ratio on OLMoE-1B-7B-0125. On WikiText-2, our method is modestly worse than EAQuant's 3-bit numbers (1.367 vs. 1.282) despite operating at one bit lower per weight; on C4, our method is modestly better (1.157 vs. 1.177). Averaging across the two datasets, the two methods are comparable — a non-trivial result given that 2-bit weight quantization is fundamentally constrained by codebook capacity and the gap between 2 and 3 bits is where rate-distortion curves typically steepen.

EAQuant also reports a W2A4 configuration (2-bit weights, 4-bit activations) on OLMoE-7B-0924, with WikiText-2 PPL 78.57 against their fp16 baseline of 7.49 — a 10.5x ratio. This is the only prior published 2-bit-weight result for OLMoE, and it is in a strictly harder regime (quantized activations) than our weight-only W2A16 setup; a direct comparison is therefore not meaningful, but the ordering of difficulty is clear: W2A16 < W2A4. Our W2A16 ratio of 1.367x / 1.157x fills a design-space cell not addressed by prior work.

Other MoE quantization work is not directly comparable to ours. MoEQuant [Hu et al., 2025] evaluates on Qwen-MoE-14B, DeepSeek-MoE-16B, and Mixtral-8x7B at 3-bit and 4-bit weight quantization; their strongest 4-bit result on DeepSeek-MoE-16B is 6.78 WikiText-2 PPL against an fp16 baseline of 6.51 (1.04x ratio at 4 bits), and the 2-bit regime is not addressed. MoPEQ [Chitty-Venkata et al., ICCVW 2025] operates on Vision-Language MoE models (DeepSeek-VL2 family and MolmoE-1B) with mixed 2/3/4-bit expert precision and is not directly comparable to our uniform 2-bit language-model setup.

**Positioning.** To our knowledge, this paper reports the first weight-only 2-bit quantization results on OLMoE in the W2A16 regime, and the first 2-bit MoE quantization results obtained entirely on consumer hardware (a single 12 GB laptop GPU). The contribution is not algorithmic novelty — BlockLDLQ and per-expert Hessian collection are small, principled variations on existing components — but methodological clarity about the right calibration unit for routed-MoE architectures and an empirical demonstration that 2-bit MoE quality preservation is achievable outside datacenter compute.

**Practical interpretation.** A 1.367x WikiText-2 ratio at 2 bits per weight means OLMoE-1B-7B retains the bulk of its language modeling quality after compression to one-eighth of its native bitrate. For comparison, the same model at fp16 has a perplexity of 6.65; our 2-bit version at 9.09 is closer to the fp16 baseline than to a model two perplexity points worse (which would correspond to a meaningfully degraded LM). The downstream task results in Section 5.2 confirm that this perplexity preservation translates into preserved zero-shot reasoning capability on standard benchmarks.
