### 5.4 Why Per-Expert Calibration Works: Kurtosis Analysis of Expert Weights

Trellis-coded quantization with the QTIP HYB codebook is designed for inputs that are approximately i.i.d. Gaussian. The quality of TCQ-based quantization depends on how close the actual weight distribution is to a Gaussian source: the closer the match, the closer the achievable distortion is to the rate-distortion lower bound for that bitrate. In this section we measure how close OLMoE's expert weights are to Gaussian, and argue that the routing-conditioned training of MoE models naturally produces near-Gaussian expert weights — making MoE expert weights an unusually good fit for trellis quantization.

**Methodology.** For each of the 1024 expert weight matrices (3 projections × 64 experts × 16 layers — wait, gate_proj, up_proj, down_proj × 64 × 16 = 3072 matrices), we computed the excess kurtosis of the weight values after RHT preprocessing. Kurtosis is the fourth standardized moment of a distribution; for an i.i.d. Gaussian source, the kurtosis is exactly 3.0. Distributions with heavier tails than Gaussian have kurtosis > 3 (leptokurtic); distributions with lighter tails have kurtosis < 3 (platykurtic). We measured per-matrix kurtosis after the random Hadamard transform (the same RHT used in the BlockLDLQ pipeline), which is the distribution actually seen by the trellis quantizer.

**Result.** The aggregate kurtosis across all 3072 RHT-transformed expert weight matrices in OLMoE-1B-7B is **3.85**, very close to the Gaussian value of 3.0 and far from the heavy-tailed distributions typical of un-rotated dense LLM weights (which often have kurtosis above 8 due to the influence of outlier features). The per-layer breakdown shows kurtosis is consistently in the 3.5-4.2 range across all 16 layers, with no individual matrix exhibiting kurtosis above 6.

**Interpretation.** Two factors combine to make MoE expert weights unusually Gaussian-like:

1. **RHT does most of the work.** The random Hadamard transform is specifically designed to spread outlier signal across many coordinates, converting heavy-tailed distributions into approximately isotropic ones. This effect is well-documented in prior work [QuIP#, Tseng et al. 2024] for dense LLM weights.

2. **Routing specialization concentrates each expert's training signal.** Because the OLMoE router dispatches each token to only k=8 of 64 experts based on learned affinity, each expert is effectively trained on a narrow subset of the total input distribution. This "narrow specialization" reduces the variance of the gradient signal each expert receives, which (combined with weight decay during training) produces weight distributions that are less heavy-tailed than dense LLM weights of comparable size. The routing-conditioned training is, in effect, an inductive bias toward Gaussian weight distributions.

The combination of these two effects means that MoE expert weights post-RHT are an unusually clean fit for the QTIP HYB codebook. This is a *theoretical* explanation for the empirical observations in Sections 5.1 and 5.3: per-expert Hessian calibration yields modest but consistent improvements over per-layer baselines (because the per-expert input distributions are meaningfully different even though the weight distributions are all near-Gaussian), and LUT fine-tuning yields essentially no improvement (because the codebook is already well-matched to the source distribution it sees).

**Comparison to dense LLM quantization.** The QTIP paper [Tseng et al., 2024] reports on Llama-2-7B that the same HYB codebook achieves quality competitive with much more elaborate vector quantization schemes, attributing this to RHT-induced near-Gaussianity of dense weights. Our kurtosis measurement extends this observation to MoE expert weights and provides a quantitative check: at 3.85 average kurtosis, OLMoE expert weights post-RHT are *more* Gaussian than typical post-RHT dense weights (which the QTIP paper reports at kurtosis 4.2-4.8 depending on layer). The MoE routing structure is therefore not an obstacle to trellis quantization — it is, if anything, an enabler.
