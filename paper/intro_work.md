OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 [Muennighoff et al. 2024] is a 7-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts language model that occupies roughly 13.8 GB in its native fp16 representation, exceeding the VRAM of most consumer laptop GPUs. Weight quantization is the standard response, and 4-bit methods such as GPTQ and AWQ are well-studied for dense models. Recent MoE-specific methods — MoEQuant, MoPEQ, EAQuant — extend these approaches to routing-based models but operate at 3-4 bits per weight. Sub-3-bit MoE quantization remains an open problem: at 2 bits per weight, uniform quantization exhibits catastrophic failure modes because the integer grid is too coarse for the tails of the weight distribution. We compress OLMoE-1B-7B to 3.3 GB on disk at 2 bits per expert weight using routing-conditioned trellis-coded quantization, achieving WikiText-2 perplexity 9.09 against an fp16 baseline of 6.65. The entire quantization research pipeline — Hessian collection, BlockLDLQ assignment, and perplexity evaluation — runs on a single NVIDIA RTX 4080 Laptop GPU (12 GB VRAM); we defer native 2-bit inference kernels to future work.

MoE expert weights are distributionally special. Because routing dispatches each token to a small subset of experts by learned affinity, each expert is effectively trained on a narrow input distribution, producing near-Gaussian weight statistics — we measure an aggregate weight kurtosis of 3.85 across OLMoE's expert layers (Section 5.4), close to the Gaussian value of 3. Near-Gaussian sources are the ideal input for trellis-coded quantization (TCQ), a technique that uses state-machine codebooks to achieve near-entropy rate-distortion performance [QTIP, Tseng et al. 2024]. TCQ has only been applied to dense LLMs. We ask whether TCQ can be extended to MoE expert weights, using the routing structure itself as the calibration signal.

We focus on OLMoE-1B-7B-0125, a fully open-source MoE LLM (weights, training data, and code released under Apache 2.0) with a standard top-8-of-64 routing architecture representative of the broader MoE family. Its 16-layer, 2048-hidden-dim structure fits in consumer hardware while being architecturally equivalent to larger MoEs like Mixtral and Qwen-MoE, suggesting the methodology should extend to those models given sufficient compute. Our pipeline has three stages: routing-conditioned per-expert Hessian collection, BlockLDLQ trellis assignment, and installation followed by perplexity evaluation — with no fine-tuning step.

This paper makes four contributions. First, we present the first application of trellis-coded quantization to any MoE model. Second, we show that routing-conditioned per-expert Hessians give a consistent improvement over a per-layer mean Hessian baseline on both perplexity benchmarks (0.12 PPL on WikiText-2, 0.27 PPL on C4), with the gap widening in deeper layers where routing specialization is strongest. Third, we demonstrate that LUT fine-tuning is unnecessary given per-expert calibration: tuning the codebook entries yields only 3.0% additional MSE reduction in expert layers and 0.0% in attention, indicating that per-expert assignment captures essentially all of the available 2-bit representational capacity. Fourth, we show that the entire pipeline runs on a single 12 GB laptop GPU with 32 GB of system RAM, demonstrating that sub-3-bit MoE quantization research is accessible without a datacenter-grade environment. On OLMoE-1B-7B-0125, we achieve WikiText-2 perplexity 9.09 (fp16: 6.65) and C4 perplexity 14.16 (fp16: 12.24) at 2 bits per weight.
