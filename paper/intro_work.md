OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 [Muennighoff et al. 2024] is a 7-billion-parameter MoE LLM that occupies roughly 13.8 GB in native fp16 representation, exceeding the VRAM of most consumer laptop GPUs. Weight quantization is the standard response, and 4-bit methods (GPTQ, AWQ) are well-studied for dense models. MoE-specific methods (MoEQuant, MoPEQ, EAQuant) extend these to routing-based models but operate at 3-4 bits. Sub-3-bit MoE quantization remains an open problem. At 2 bits per weight, uniform quantization methods exhibit catastrophic failure modes because the grid is too coarse for the weight distribution tails. We reduce OLMoE-1B-7B-0125 to 3.3 GB at 2 bits per expert weight using routing-conditioned trellis-coded quantization, achieving WikiText-2 perplexity 9.09 against an fp16 baseline of 6.65, with the entire quantization pipeline running on a single NVIDIA RTX 4080 Laptop GPU (12 GB VRAM).

MoE expert weights are distributionally special. Because routing dispatches each token to a small subset of experts by learned affinity, each expert is effectively trained on a narrow input distribution, producing near-Gaussian weight statistics. We measure an aggregate weight kurtosis of 3.85 across OLMoE's expert layers (Section 5.4), close to the Gaussian value of 3.
Near-Gaussian sources are the ideal input for trellis-coded quantization (TCQ), which uses state-machine codebooks to achieve near-entropy rate-distortion performance [QTIP, Tseng et al. 2024]. TCQ has only been applied to dense LLMs. We ask: can TCQ be applied to MoE expert weights, using the routing structure to calibrate per-expert rather than per-layer?

We focus on OLMoE-1B-7B-0125, a fully open-source MoE LLM (weights, training data, and code released under Apache 2.0) with a standard top-8-of-64 routing architecture representative of the broader MoE family. OLMoE's 16-layer, 2048-hidden-dim structure fits in consumer hardware while being architecturally equivalent to larger MoEs like Mixtral and Qwen-MoE, suggesting the methodology should extend to those models given sufficient compute.

We present a three-stage pipeline: routing-conditioned per-expert Hessian collection, BlockLDLQ trellis assignment with per-expert H, and install-and-evaluate with no LUT fine-tuning.

Our contributions are: (1) the first application of trellis-coded quantization to any MoE model; (2) a per-expert Hessian collection methodology that improves 2-bit MoE quantization over per-layer mean H by ~X% [TODO after A.11]; (3) a demonstration that LUT fine-tuning is unnecessary given per-expert calibration, with LUT-tuning yielding only 3.0% MSE reduction in experts and 0.0% in attention; (4) the entire pipeline running on a 12 GB VRAM laptop GPU with 32 GB of system RAM

On OLMoE-1B-7B-0125, we achieve wikitext-2 perplexity 9.09 (fp16: 6.65) and C4 perplexity 14.16 (fp16: 12.24) at 2 bits per weight.

At 2 bits per weight, the information content of OLMoE-1B-7B-0125's expert layers is approximately 1.68 GB; combined with fp16 embeddings, LM head, and ancillary layers, the theoretical minimum footprint is ~2.1 GB. Our reference implementation currently stores quantized weights at one nibble per byte (3.3 GB on disk) and dequantizes to fp16 at installation time for evaluation; we defer bit-packed storage and native trellis inference kernels to future work [citation: Paper 2 when ready]. With these additions, the model would fit within the memory envelope of edge inference devices including 4 GB ARM SBCs.
